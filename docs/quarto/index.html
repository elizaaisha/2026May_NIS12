Trends and In-Hospital Outcomes of Pregnancy-Associated Spontaneous Coronary Artery Dissection in the United States: A Nationwide Inpatient Sample Analysis, 2017–2022

Author
Affiliation

Eliza Aisha

Dow University of Health Sciences

Preamble:

  • Background: Spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD) is an important cause of acute myocardial infarction in young women and is strongly associated with pregnancy and the puerperium. Pregnancy-associated SCAD is linked to substantial maternal morbidity and mortality due to hormonal, hemodynamic, and vascular changes unique to pregnancy. Despite its clinical significance, SCAD remains underrecognized in obstetric patients and contemporary nationwide data are limited.

  • Clinical Significance: Cardiovascular disease is a leading contributor to maternal morbidity and mortality in the United States. Improved understanding of the epidemiology, inpatient outcomes, and healthcare utilization associated with pregnancy-related SCAD may help inform cardio-obstetric care pathways and risk stratification strategies.

  • Data Source: Cross-sectional analysis of the National Inpatient Sample (NIS) from 2017 to 2022.

  • Inclusion Criteria:

    • Female patients aged 15–54 years
    • Pregnancy-related hospitalization identified using ICD-10 obstetric codes
    • Concurrent diagnosis of SCAD (I25.42)

  • Outcomes:

    • In-hospital all-cause mortality

    • Percutaneous coronary intervention

    • Cardiogenic Shock

    • Mechanical ventilation

    • Acute kidney injury

    • Cardaic arrest

    • Ventricular arrhythmia

    • Blood transfusion

    • Congestive heart failure

    • Non home discharge

    • Total hospital length of stay (days)

    • Total charge, inflation adjusted to 2017 ($)

  • Covariates for Adjustment: All models adjusted for the following

    • Demographics: Age, socioeconomic status and insurance, race, hospital region, hospital bedsize, hospital location, and teaching status.

    • Medical History: Hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, liver disease, obesity, anemia, depression, smoking, cardiomyopathy, coagulopathy, gestational hypertension, gestational diabetes.

  • Statistical Methods:

    • Univariable Analysis:

      • Categorical variables: Compared using Pearson’s chi-square test with Rao & Scott adjustment

      • Continuous variables: Analyzed using the design-based Kruskal–Wallis test, a non-parametric alternative suitable for survey-weighted data

    • Multivariable Analysis:

      • Binary outcomes (e.g., mortality): Survey-weighted logistic regression using the quasibinomial family

      • Continuous outcomes (e.g., LOS, hospital charges): Survey-weighted linear regression using the gaussian family

      • Results reported as adjusted odds ratios (aORs) or beta coefficients with 95% confidence intervals

  • Software: All statistical analyses were performed using the R Statistical Language (Version 4.5.0; R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria), incorporating survey-weighted procedures via the survey package to account for the complex sampling design of NIS.
